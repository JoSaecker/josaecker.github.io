We study heterogeneous firms' decision-making for investment into greening the production process. Empirically, we find, first, that firms primarily use their own funds for financing greening investment, that, second, more productive firms have a higher propensity to engage in greening investment and that, third, firms facing more competition invest less. We incorporate these three stylized facts into a heterogeneous firm model where a firm's decision to engage in greening investment depends positively on idiosyncratic firm productivity. Competition by non-greening firms laggards depresses profits, own funds and thus relatively increases greening costs and decreases the likelihood of greening. In the dynamic model exercise, we analyse the effect of a policymaker outlining an uncertain path for a future greening of the production process. The optimal dynamic greening path depends thus on the trade off between temporary output losses and a higher share of greening firms. A low transition policy probability will, further, endogenously reduce the current share of greening firms via keeping competition by non-greeners high.
